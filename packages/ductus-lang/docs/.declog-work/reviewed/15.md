15-1. A connection is a directional, typed link between two node instances. (§13.6.0)
15-2. Connections are first-class entities: each has its own identity. (§13.6.0)
15-3. A connection carries its own reactive content — attrs, recurrents, deriveds — per-relationship state belonging to the relationship rather than to either endpoint: `Drives` holding `attr aggressiveness`. (§13.6.0)
15-4. A connection type may satisfy traits. (§13.6.0)
15-5. Every connection has a source — the `from` endpoint — and a destination — the `to` endpoint. (§13.6.0)
15-6. A caller-placed connection participates in the source node's outgoing surface declared via `outgoing:`. (§13.6.0)
15-7. A self-sourced connection sits outside the source's `outgoing:` surface. (§13.6.0)
15-8. Caller-placed and self-sourced connections both count at the destination node's `incoming:` surface. (§13.6.0)
15-9. The `from` endpoint is the enclosing instance, fixed structurally by where the connection is placed; it does not vary at runtime. (§13.6.0)
15-10. The `to` endpoint is a node reference and may be a reactive value that re-points among existing nodes at runtime. (§13.6.0)
15-11. When `to` re-points, the connection instance persists and retains its per-relationship state; only its target moves. (§13.6.0)
15-12. Re-pointing neither creates nor drops a node — a connection never owns its endpoints. (§13.6.0)
15-13. A node declares which connection types callers may wire to and from it via `incoming:` and `outgoing:` clauses, with optional cardinality constraints and the `dynamic` supply-mode marker. (§13.6.0)
15-14. Connection instances appear at placement, not in `incoming:`/`outgoing:` declarations. (§13.6.0)
15-15. A connection placement is an exposition entry at a position in its source's ordered structural sequence. (§13.6.0)
15-16. A connection's exposition position is where it engages during traversal. (§13.6.0)
15-17. Every statically placed connection exists from construction; only a dynamic destination's target may move. (§13.6.0)
15-18. A connection's exposition entry belongs to the source's exposition, yet the connection is owned by no single endpoint. (§13.6.0)
15-19. A node cannot store a direct reference to another node instance: `attr target: SomeNode` ✗. (§13.6.0)
15-20. A node-instance reference is a borrow, and a borrow may not be stored in a cell. (§13.6.0)
15-21. Storing a node type rather than an instance — a template slot — uses `Type[…]`. (§13.6.0)
15-22. A connection declares its endpoint types in exactly one of three forms: single, cartesian, or pairs. (§13.6.1)
15-23. Mixing endpoint forms is a compile error: `pairs:` alongside `from:`+`to:` ✗. (§13.6.1)
15-24. Single form requires `from:` exactly once and `to:` exactly once: `from: Driver` / `to: Drivable`. (§13.6.1.1)
15-25. A connection body admits `when:` (activation predicate), `const`, `signal`, `attr`, `default attr`, `recurrent[N]`, `derived`, and `stream policy[N]` declarations. (§13.6.1.1)
15-26. A connection `const` is a per-type compile-time constant. (§13.6.1.1)
15-27. Connection `signal`, `attr`, `recurrent`, `derived`, and `stream` members are per-instance. (§13.6.1.1)
15-28. A connection type may declare at most one `default attr`. (§13.6.1.1)
15-29. At placement, `/expr` targets the connection's default attr. (§13.6.1.1)
15-30. A placement's destination endpoint is supplied in the placement body, never via `/expr`. (§13.6.1.1)
15-31. `from` and `to` are endpoint slots, not attributes; placement attribute syntax (`name=value`, flags) does not target them. (§13.6.1.1)
15-32. In single form, `from` and `to` resolve inside the body directly to the endpoint instances at their concrete types: `to.top_speed`. (§13.6.1.1)
15-33. Cartesian form lists multiple from-types and/or to-types; every from×to combination is a valid placement: `from: Person, Company` with `to: Vehicle, Property`. (§13.6.1.2)
15-34. A cartesian body is specialized per from×to combination by a required, statically resolved `match`: `match (from, to):`. (§13.6.1.2)
15-35. The cartesian `match` must be exhaustive over the statically-known from×to product — a compile-time check. (§13.6.1.2)
15-36. Within each cartesian arm, `from` and `to` are concretely typed: `(Person(p), Vehicle(v)):`. (§13.6.1.2)
15-37. Cartesian form introduces no combined runtime value and no new type former. (§13.6.1.2)
15-38. Pairs form lists explicit `From -> To` combinations; only listed pairs are valid placements: `pairs:` with `Driver -> Vehicle`. (§13.6.1.3)
15-39. In pairs form, endpoints are accessed only via `pair`, matched per declared pair: `match pair: (Driver(d), Vehicle(v)): ...`. (§13.6.1.3)
15-40. In pairs form, `from` and `to` are not independently accessible. (§13.6.1.3)
15-41. Listing the same `From -> To` pair twice is a compile error. (§13.6.1.3)
15-42. Asymmetric pair counts are legal — pair uniqueness, not type count, governs: `A -> X`, `A -> Y`, `B -> Y` ✓. (§13.6.1.3)
15-43. A pairs-form body is uniform across pairs; attrs/deriveds cannot vary by pair. (§13.6.1.3)
15-44. Differing per-pair content requires a separate connection type per pair. (§13.6.1.3)
15-45. `from`, `to`, and `pair` are bound at the connection's placement, which specifies the source (the enclosing instance) and the destination. (§13.6.2)
15-46. The `from` binding is fixed for the connection's lifetime. (§13.6.2)
15-47. When the destination is a reactive selection or a `Handle`, `to` follows whichever node it currently resolves to. (§13.6.2)
15-48. Body reads of `to.*` re-evaluate when the destination re-points — a dynamic dependency on the current target. (§13.6.2)
15-49. Inside a connection body, `from` and `to` are never `Option`; they are the live endpoint instances directly. (§13.6.2)
15-50. A `Handle` destination (read type `Option[&N]`) is unwrapped at the boundary: while it resolves to `Some`, the connection is active and the body sees the contained node as `to`. (§13.6.2)
15-51. While a `Handle` destination resolves to `None`, the connection freezes and its body does not run at all. (§13.6.2)
15-52. The freeze is the unwrap: connection body code never handles destination absence. (§13.6.2)
15-53. Gates and the unresolved-destination condition are the only switches on a connection's reactive liveness. (§13.6.2)
15-54. Traversal position never switches a connection's liveness: an unengaged or already-passed connection keeps computing. (§13.6.2)
15-55. `from` and `to` are body-internal, visible only inside the connection type's own body; there is no external `some_conn.to` access. (§13.6.2)
15-56. A connection does not surface its endpoints or its activation as readable fields. (§13.6.2)
15-57. An outside observer reads a connection's own cells (attrs, deriveds), which hold their last committed value even while the connection is frozen. (§13.6.2)
15-58. Liveness-awareness about a node is the job of a `Handle`, not of a connection's surface. (§13.6.2)
15-59. A connection may declare generic parameters: `connection Contains[T]:` with `from: Container[T]`, `to: T`. (§13.6.3)
15-60. A connection's generic parameters scope over its `from`, `to`, attrs, recurrents, and deriveds. (§13.6.3)
15-61. Each unique instantiation of a generic connection is a distinct connection type. (§13.6.3)
15-62. A connection body contains no `fn` declarations. (§13.6.4)
15-63. Functions on connections are free functions taking the connection type, dispatched via uniform call syntax. (§13.6.4)
15-64. Trait methods on connections are implemented in `fulfill` blocks. (§13.6.4)
15-65. A connection body contains no `repeat` declarations. (§13.6.4)
15-66. Dynamic-scope structure belongs in `expose:` blocks, placement bodies, or effect `desired:` blocks, never in connection bodies. (§13.6.4)
15-67. A connection body hosts no effect instantiations. (§13.6.4)
15-68. Effects are instantiated only in a node's `effects:` clause. (§13.6.4)
15-69. A connection holds no outside-world resource of its own. (§13.6.4)
15-70. `Circularity` is a language-defined marker trait with no methods: `trait Circularity`. (§13.6.5)
15-71. A connection type opts into participating in topology cycles by declaring `satisfies Circularity`. (§13.6.5)
15-72. Every topology cycle in the construction-time node graph must traverse at least one connection whose type satisfies `Circularity`. (§13.6.5)
15-73. A cycle consisting only of non-`Circularity` connections is a compile error. (§13.6.5)
15-74. Satisfying `Circularity` has no effect beyond opting the connection type into cycle participation; that is its sole purpose. (§13.6.5)
15-75. Which connection types satisfy `Circularity` is domain-defined. (§13.6.5)
15-76. Name resolution proceeds outward through enclosing scopes, innermost first — standard lexical scoping. (§13.7)
15-77. A node or connection body is an ordinary scope; its members are in scope within the body's reactive expressions. (§13.7)
15-78. There is no receiver concept: a bare name binds to the nearest enclosing scope that declares it. (§13.7)
15-79. Two explicit scope anchors exist: `here::x` resolves in the current scope, `module::x` in the module top level. (§13.7)
15-80. Routine member access uses a bare name or `here::`; `subject` is reserved for the value role. (§13.7)
15-81. Within a node or connection body, the scope chain innermost-first is: local bindings, the instance body scope, the module top-level scope. (§13.7.1)
15-82. Local bindings comprise `let` bindings and `for`-loop variables inside a reactive expression. (§13.7.1)
15-83. The instance body scope comprises `attr`/`signal`/`recurrent`/`derived`/`stream` cells, `parts`, and the reserved fields `from`, `to`, `incoming`, `outgoing`, `pair`, `exposition`. (§13.7.1)
15-84. The module top-level scope comprises module-level `signal`, `derived`, `recurrent`, `stream`, `const`, and `let` declarations. (§13.7.1)
15-85. A bare name resolves to the nearest scope in the chain that declares it: `local_gain * master_gain` reads a member and a module cell anchor-free. (§13.7.1)
15-86. `here::x` resolves `x` in the current scope, bypassing inner local bindings and ignoring same-named module-level declarations. (§13.7.2)
15-87. `here::` is meaningful in any scope (function, operator, trait, node/connection body), always denoting the binding in that very scope rather than an outer one. (§13.7.2)
15-88. Type-level positions (trait declarations, `fulfill` blocks) use the `Subject` alias for the subject type, not `here::`. (§13.7.2)
15-89. Bare and `here::`-anchored references resolve to the same cell when there is no collision: `expertise_level` and `here::expertise_level`. (§13.7.2)
15-90. `module::x` resolves `x` in the enclosing module's top-level scope, bypassing body and local scopes: `on module::tick:`. (§13.7.3)
15-91. `module::` reaches only the current module's top level; cross-module access uses the module-path mechanism. (§13.7.3)
15-92. A bare name declared in both the instance body scope and the module top-level scope is ambiguous — a compile error: `derived a: f32 = gain` ✗. (§13.7.4)
15-93. The body/module collision is resolved by anchoring: `here::gain` selects the member, `module::gain` the module-level cell. (§13.7.4)
15-94. Local bindings (`let`, `for`-vars) shadow outer scopes normally; the ambiguity error applies only to the body-vs-module collision. (§13.7.4)
15-95. The reserved fields `from`, `to`, `incoming`, `outgoing`, `pair`, `parts`, and `exposition` are instance-body-scope members resolving by bare name in expression position: `incoming.ShowsCount[0].count`. (§13.7.5)
15-96. Reserved-field keywords carry clause-header meaning only in declaration position (statement level, trailing colon: `from:`). (§13.7.5)
15-97. In expression-operand position, a reserved-field keyword denotes the corresponding instance field: `from.expertise_level`. (§13.7.5)
15-98. Reserved words cannot be declared as cell names, so reserved fields never collide with user names. (§13.7.5)
15-99. `here::` remains available as the explicit form for reserved fields: `here::from`, `here::incoming`. (§13.7.5)
15-100. `in` is not a reserved instance field; it serves solely as the `for`-loop separator: `for x in incoming.ShowsCount`. (§13.7.5)
15-101. A cell reference — bare, `here::`-anchored, or `module::`-anchored — joins the reactive dependency graph: `derived x: f32 = y + 1` dirties `x` when the resolved `y` changes. (§13.7.6)
15-102. Dependency edges are emitted per instance: body-scope references resolve to that specific instance's cells, never another instance's. (§13.7.6)
15-103. `module::` references resolve to the single shared module-level cell. (§13.7.6)
15-104. `subject` is the instance value, available in node/connection bodies for handling the whole instance as a value: `total_output(subject)`. (§13.7.7)
15-105. `subject.some_method()` and `some_method(subject)` are the same call — dispatch is by first argument and the dot is sugar, not a receiver binding. (§13.7.7)
15-106. There is no implicit receiver: the instance is always explicit, as `subject` in a body or as a named parameter in a `fulfill`-block function. (§13.7.7)
15-107. A type's "methods" are ordinary scoped functions taking the instance as a developer-named first parameter: `fn display(value: Subject)`. (§13.7.7)
15-108. `Subject` is the type-level alias for the subject type, usable only in type positions in trait declarations and `fulfill` blocks. (§13.7.7)
15-109. `here` denotes the current (innermost) scope as a namespace, usable only as the left side of `::`; it is not a value and has no `.` form. (§13.7.7)
15-110. `module` denotes the enclosing module namespace, usable only as the left side of `::`; it is not a value and has no `.` form. (§13.7.7)
15-111. The four reserved identifiers never overlap in role: `subject` is a value usable in expression positions, `Subject` a type usable in type positions, `here` and `module` namespaces usable only as the left side of `::`. (§13.7.7)

FINDINGS:
15-F1. Liveness tension between §13.6.0 and §13.6.2. §13.6.0 states statically placed connections are "always reactively live" (citing §13.3.7.6), while §13.6.2 states that gates and the unresolved-destination condition switch a connection's reactive liveness off — a frozen connection's "body does not run at all". Two defensible readings of §13.6.0's "always": (a) engagement-scoped — live regardless of traversal/engagement position, subject to §13.6.2's two switches as the only exceptions; (b) literal/unconditional — contradicting §13.6.2 and the §13.9.7 freeze it cites. Reading (a) is better supported (the §13.6.0 sentence contrasts schema presence with engagement ordering, cites the traversal section, and §13.6.2 explicitly says "the only switches ... traversal position never is"), but the drafter adopted it silently with zero recorded findings. The uncontested §13.6.2 statements stand as entries 15-53 and 15-54. quarantined: "A statically placed connection is reactively live regardless of traversal/engagement position; its exposition position orders only its engagement. (§13.6.0)" — reinstate under reading (a); under reading (b) it would conflict with 15-51 and 15-53.
15-F2. Reserved-field roster mismatch between §13.7.1 and §13.7.5. §13.7.1 lists `parts` separately from "the reserved endpoint/structure fields" (`from`, `to`, `incoming`, `outgoing`, `pair`, `exposition`), while §13.7.5 includes `parts` inside its reserved-fields roster (`from`, `to`, `incoming`, `outgoing`, `pair`, `parts`, `exposition`). The union and the normative effects (all resolve as instance-body members; reserved words not declarable as cell names) are consistent either way, but whether `parts` belongs to the named "reserved fields" set differs between the two enumerations. Entries 15-83 and 15-95 each faithfully reproduce their own section's roster and both are retained. quarantined: none
